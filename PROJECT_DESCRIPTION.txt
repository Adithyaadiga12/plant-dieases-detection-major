"AgroVision AI is a smartphone app that lets any farmer photograph a plant leaf
and instantly find out what disease it has — even without internet. The app uses
a deep learning model trained on 87,848 leaf images to identify 38 different
plant diseases across 14 crops, with 98.3% accuracy. Results are shown in the
farmer's local language (English, Kannada, Hindi, Tamil, or Telugu) and read
aloud by voice."

  SECTION 2 — THE PROBLEM
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Plant diseases cause 20–40% of global crop yield loss every year. In India, this
directly impacts millions of small farmers who cannot afford to lose their crops.

The existing problems:
  • Farmers cannot identify plant diseases by looking at leaves — it requires
    expert knowledge that most farmers simply do not have.
  • Agricultural experts and extension officers are too few and spread too thin
    to reach every farmer in time.
  • Online tools require internet — rural farms often have no connectivity.
  • Existing apps are English-only — illiterate or non-English-speaking farmers
    cannot use them.
  • By the time a disease is identified, it has often already spread.

What we needed:
  → A tool that works offline, speaks the farmer's language, gives instant
    results, and runs on a basic Android phone.

  SECTION 3 — OUR SOLUTION (SIMPLE LANGUAGE)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

We built a smartphone app called AgroVision AI that works like this:

  STEP 1 — The farmer opens the app and takes a photo of a plant leaf using
           their phone camera (or picks an existing photo from gallery).

  STEP 2 — The app checks whether the photo is good enough — is it blurry?
           Too dark? Does it actually show a leaf? If not, it asks to retake.

  STEP 3 — An AI model running entirely inside the phone analyzes the photo.
           No photo is sent to any server. Everything happens on the device.
           This takes about 1–2 seconds.

  STEP 4 — The app shows:
           • The disease name (e.g., "Tomato Late Blight")
           • How confident the AI is (e.g., 94.7%)
           • Severity level (Mild / Moderate / Severe)
           • Symptoms to look for
           • What causes the disease
           • How to prevent it
           • How to treat / remedy it
           All of this in the farmer's chosen language.

  STEP 5 — The result is also READ ALOUD by the phone in the farmer's language,
           so even an illiterate farmer can understand it.

The app also keeps a history of all past scans, has a searchable library of all
38 diseases, and allows the farmer to switch between 5 Indian languages.

  SECTION 4 — THE DATASET
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

We used the PlantVillage Dataset — one of the most widely used public datasets
for plant disease research.

  Total images   :  87,848 leaf photographs
  Number of classes: 38  (diseases + healthy states)
  Crop species   :  14  (Apple, Grape, Tomato, Potato, Corn, Pepper, etc.)
  Image type     :  Controlled background, color leaf photos (JPEG)
  Source         :  Penn State University, publicly available

The 38 classes include both diseased and healthy leaves. For example:
  - Tomato___Early_blight
  - Tomato___Late_blight
  - Tomato___healthy
  - Apple___Apple_scab
  - Grape___Black_rot
  ... and 33 more.

How we split the data (80/10/10 rule):
  Training set   :  70,278 images  (used to teach the model)
  Validation set :  8,785  images  (used during training to check progress)
  Test set       :  8,785  images  (used ONLY at the end to measure accuracy)

The split is done at the class level (stratified), meaning each of the 38
classes has images in all three splits.

  SECTION 5 — THE AI MODEL (TECHNICAL)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

We designed a custom dual-backbone convolutional neural network (CNN) that
combines the strengths of two well-known deep learning models.

─── What is a CNN? ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

A CNN (Convolutional Neural Network) is a type of AI that learns to recognize
visual patterns in images. It scans an image in small patches (called kernels),
detecting edges, textures, shapes, and eventually whole objects — in our case,
disease patterns on leaves.

─── Our Model Architecture ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────

INPUT: A leaf image, resized to 224×224 pixels, 3 color channels (RGB).
       Each pixel value is normalized: (pixel ÷ 127.5) − 1.0  → range [−1, +1]
       This matches exactly how the model was trained.

The image is fed into TWO separate neural networks at the same time:

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  BACKBONE 1: EfficientNetB0                                             │
  │  • 5.3 million parameters                                               │
  │  • Uses "MBConv blocks" — compound scaled, efficient architecture       │
  │  • Pre-trained on ImageNet (1.2 million general images) — knows shapes, │
  │    textures, colors before it even sees a leaf                          │
  │  • Final layer: Global Average Pooling → outputs 1,280 features         │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  BACKBONE 2: MobileNetV2                                                │
  │  • 3.4 million parameters                                               │
  │  • Uses "Inverted Residual Blocks" — lightweight, fast on mobile        │
  │  • Also pre-trained on ImageNet                                         │
  │  • Final layer: Global Average Pooling → outputs 1,280 features         │
  └─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

Why use TWO backbones?
  EfficientNetB0 is more accurate but heavier.
  MobileNetV2 is faster and lighter.
  Together, they extract DIFFERENT features from the same image, making the
  combined model more robust than either alone.

After both backbones process the image, their outputs are COMBINED:

  1,280 features (EfficientNetB0) + 1,280 features (MobileNetV2)
                        = 2,560 combined features

This combined vector then goes through the Classification Head:

  ┌────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  Batch Normalization                               │
  │  Dense layer: 512 neurons, ReLU activation        │
  │  Dropout: 40%  (randomly disables neurons during  │
  │           training to prevent memorization)       │
  │  Dense layer: 256 neurons, ReLU activation        │
  │  Dropout: 30%                                      │
  │  Dense layer: 38 neurons, Softmax activation      │
  └────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘

The SOFTMAX output gives a probability for each of the 38 classes.
The class with the highest probability is the prediction.
Example: [0.001, 0.003, ..., 0.947, ..., 0.002] → class 29 = 94.7% confident.

─── Total Parameters ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  EfficientNetB0     :  ~5.3 M parameters
  MobileNetV2        :  ~3.4 M parameters
  Classification Head:  ~1.6 M parameters
  Total              :  ~10.3 M parameters

  SECTION 6 — HOW WE TRAINED THE MODEL
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

Training was done on KAGGLE (free cloud GPU — NVIDIA T4).

─── Data Pipeline ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We used TensorFlow's tf.data pipeline (NOT the older ImageDataGenerator):
  • Images are decoded and resized IN PARALLEL using multiple CPU cores
  • Batches of 64 images are prefetched while the GPU is processing the
    previous batch — this eliminates GPU idle time
  • Result: 3–5× faster training than the traditional approach

Data Augmentation (only applied to training images, not test):
  • Random horizontal flip
  • Random vertical flip
  • Random brightness change (±15%)
  • All augmented values clipped to [−1, +1]
  This makes the model see each leaf from different angles and lighting,
  which helps it generalize to real-world phone photos.

Mixed Float16 Precision:
  • By default, GPUs compute in 32-bit floating point
  • Mixed precision uses 16-bit floats where safe → 2–3× faster on T4 GPU
  • Critical: the final Softmax layer is kept in float32 for numerical stability

─── Two-Phase Training Strategy ─────────────────────────────────────────────────

PHASE 1 — Feature Transfer (8 epochs):
  • Both backbones are FROZEN (their weights don't change)
  • Only the Classification Head is trained from scratch
  • Optimizer: Adam with learning rate 0.001
  • The model learns to use pre-trained backbone features for our leaf data
  • Training time: ~10–12 minutes on T4

PHASE 2 — Fine-Tuning (4 epochs):
  • The top 20 layers of EACH backbone are UNFROZEN
  • All layers are trained together at a very slow learning rate (0.00005)
  • The backbones fine-tune their features specifically for plant diseases
  • Optimizer: Adam with learning rate 0.00005
  • Training time: ~5–7 minutes on T4

Callbacks used during training:
  • ModelCheckpoint: saves the model only when validation accuracy improves
  • EarlyStopping: stops training if no improvement for 3 epochs (saves time)
  • ReduceLROnPlateau: halves the learning rate if validation loss stops improving

─── TFLite Conversion ───────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

After training, the model is in Keras format (.keras, ~50 MB).
To run on a phone, we convert it to TFLite (TensorFlow Lite):

  Problem encountered: The model was trained in mixed_float16 precision, which
  bakes float16 operations into the saved graph. Converting this directly to
  TFLite causes errors (double-quantization bug).

  Solution we implemented:
  1. Load the trained model and extract its weights (582 weight tensors)
  2. Delete the trained model from memory
  3. Rebuild the EXACT same architecture in pure float32
  4. Copy all the trained weights into the new float32 model
  5. Convert THAT model to TFLite float16

  Result:
  • Format: TFLite float16 quantization
  • File size: 14.8 MB (down from ~50 MB Keras model)
  • Input shape: [1, 224, 224, 3]
  • Output shape: [1, 38]
  • Inference time on phone: ~300–500 ms per image

─── How Inference Works in the App ─────────────────────────────────────────────

  STEP A — Image Quality Gate (ImageQualityService):
    • File size check:    must be < 15 MB
    • Dimension check:    must be ≥ 100 × 100 pixels
    • Brightness check:   average pixel brightness must be 20–245
                          (rejects photos taken in total darkness or
                          overexposed/washed-out photos)
    • Blur check:         Laplacian variance must be > 8
                          (rejects shaky/unfocused photos)
    • Leaf detection:     HSV color analysis — at least 2% of pixels must
                          match the hue/saturation/value range of either
                          healthy leaves (green: hue 60–165°) or diseased
                          tissue (yellow/brown: hue 25–70°)
    If ANY check fails → show error dialog, ask user to retake.

  STEP B — Pre-processing (TfliteService):
    1. Decode the image file bytes using the 'image' Dart package
    2. Apply EXIF orientation (bakeOrientation) — fixes photos that are
       sideways or upside-down due to phone orientation at capture time
    3. Resize to exactly 224×224 pixels using bilinear interpolation
    4. For each pixel: red, green, blue channels are normalized:
       value = (pixel ÷ 127.5) − 1.0
       This maps pixel range [0, 255] → [−1.0, +1.0]
       THIS IS CRITICAL — the model was trained with this exact formula.
       Using any other normalization gives wrong predictions.
    5. Values stored as Float32List of length 224×224×3 = 150,528 floats

  STEP C — TFLite Inference:
    1. Input tensor shape was pre-set to [1, 224, 224, 3] at app startup
    2. setTo(inputF32) — writes the 150,528 floats to the input tensor
    3. invoke() — runs the forward pass through the neural network
    4. copyTo(outputBuffer) — reads the [1, 38] output tensor
    Result: 38 probability values, one per disease class, summing to ~1.0

  STEP D — Post-Processing:
    1. Find the index with the highest probability (argmax)
    2. If highest probability < 40% → return "Not Recognized" result
    3. If ≥ 40% → map index to class name using our class_names list
    4. Severity classification:
       • Confidence < 75%  → Mild
       • Confidence 75–90% → Moderate
       • Confidence ≥ 90%  → Severe
       • Healthy class     → No severity

─── Multilingual System ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Languages supported: English (en), Kannada (kn), Hindi (hi),
                       Tamil (ta), Telugu (te)

  How it works:
  • All UI strings are stored in a single Dart map (app_strings.dart)
    with 60+ keys per language
  • LocaleService is a ChangeNotifier singleton that holds the current language
  • When language changes, LocaleService notifies all listeners
  • The entire MaterialApp is rebuilt via ListenableBuilder
  • ALL text in ALL 6 screens updates instantly, no restart needed
  • The selected language is persisted in SharedPreferences so it survives
    app restarts

  TTS (Voice):
  • flutter_tts package reads the result aloud
  • Language code passed to TTS: en-US, kn-IN, hi-IN, ta-IN, te-IN
  • If device doesn't have the selected language installed, falls back to
    English automatically
  • User can disable TTS completely from Settings screen

─── Local Database ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

  Hive (scan history):
  • Each scan is saved as a ScanHistory object with:
    - imagePath (path to saved photo)
    - prediction (disease display name)
    - confidence (0.0–1.0)
    - scannedAt (DateTime)
    - isHealthy (true/false)
  • Stored in a Hive box — ultra-fast, file-based, no SQL
  • Maximum 50 entries (oldest deleted when limit reached)

  SQLite (treatment database):
  • All 38 disease entries are seeded into a 'treatments' table at first launch
  • Table: id, rawClass, name, description, symptoms, causes, prevention, remedy
  • Used by Disease Library screen for searchable browsing
  • Read-only after seeding — no user writes to this database

  SECTION 9 — RESULTS AND ACCURACY
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  Inference Speed  :  ~300–500 ms per image on Android phone
  Model Size       :  14.8 MB (Float16 TFLite)
  Confidence Threshold: Results shown only when model is > 40% confident.
                        Below this → "Not Recognized, try a clearer photo"

  Severity thresholds (for diseased leaves):
  • 40–75% confidence  → Mild (possibly early-stage infection)
  • 75–90% confidence  → Moderate (established infection, act soon)
  • ≥ 90% confidence   → Severe (advanced infection, immediate action needed)
  • Healthy class      → No severity level shown

  SECTION 10 — KEY INNOVATIONS / WHAT MAKES IT SPECIAL
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  1. DUAL BACKBONE ARCHITECTURE
     Most similar apps use a single CNN. We combine EfficientNetB0 and
     MobileNetV2 in parallel, extracting different feature representations
     from the same image and merging them before classification.
     This improves robustness without adding too much model size.

  2. FULLY OFFLINE OPERATION
     The 14.8 MB TFLite model is embedded inside the app itself.
     Zero network calls are made during inference. This is critical for
     real-world use in Indian villages with poor connectivity.

  3. REGIONAL LANGUAGE SUPPORT WITH VOICE
     Most plant disease apps are English-only.
     We support Kannada, Hindi, Tamil, and Telugu — 4 of India's most spoken
     regional languages — with full UI translation AND voice output.

  4. INTELLIGENT IMAGE QUALITY GATE
     Before running inference, we reject low-quality images (too dark, blurry,
     not showing a leaf). This prevents the AI from giving wrong results on
     random photos — a common failure in deployed AI systems.

  5. COMPLETE DISEASE INFORMATION
     The app doesn't just name the disease. For all 38 classes it provides:
     symptoms, causes, prevention methods, and specific remedies.
     This information is stored locally in SQLite — no internet needed.

  6. PRODUCTION-READY ARCHITECTURE
     The app uses proper software architecture:
     - Singleton service pattern (TFLite, locale, history, database)
     - ChangeNotifier + ListenableBuilder for reactive UI
     - Layered: Presentation → Service → Data → ML Engine

  SECTION 11 — CHALLENGES WE SOLVED
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  CHALLENGE 1: TFLite model crashing with "CONV_2D failed: input->dims->size
  != 4 (1 != 4)"
    Root cause: Passing a flat Float32List to run() made TFLite interpret the
    input shape as [150528] (1D) instead of [1,224,224,3] (4D).
    Fix: Explicitly pre-resize the input tensor at model load time using
    resizeInputTensor(0, [1,224,224,3]) and allocateTensors(), then use
    setTo() / invoke() / copyTo() instead of run().

  CHALLENGE 2: "Output shape mismatch: [1,38] vs [38]"
    Root cause: The model's output tensor is 2D [1,38], but we were trying to
    copy into a 1D Float32List(38), which fails the strict shape check.
    Fix: Use List.generate(1, (_) => List<double>.filled(38, 0.0)) as the
    output buffer, then read outputBuffer[0].

  CHALLENGE 3: TFLite conversion failing with "ERROR_NEEDS_FLEX_OPS" for
  Conv2D, Relu6, MatMul
    Root cause: The model was trained with mixed_float16 precision, which
    bakes float16 operations into the saved graph. Direct conversion to
    TFLite float16 causes double-quantization errors.
    Fix: Extract all weights from the trained model, rebuild the identical
    architecture in pure float32 from scratch, copy weights, then convert.

  CHALLENGE 4: Training too slow (25+ minutes per epoch on Kaggle)
    Root cause: Using ImageDataGenerator (pure-Python, CPU-only) with a
    complex dual-backbone model.
    Fix: Replaced with tf.data pipeline — parallel JPEG decode across all
    CPU cores, GPU-prefetched batches. Result: ~2–3 min/epoch instead of 25+.

  CHALLENGE 5: Changing language not updating the entire UI
    Root cause: Flutter's standard localization (flutter_localizations) uses
    static delegates and doesn't support hot-swapping language at runtime
    while keeping the same MaterialApp instance.
    Fix: Built a custom LocaleService as a ChangeNotifier. Wrapped MaterialApp
    with ListenableBuilder(listenable: LocaleService.instance) so the entire
    widget tree rebuilds when language changes.

  LIKELY QUESTIONS AND ANSWERS:

  Q: Why did you use TWO networks instead of one?
  A: Each network learns to see the leaf slightly differently. EfficientNetB0
     focuses on compound-scaled feature patterns; MobileNetV2 focuses on
     lightweight inverted residual features. Their combined representation
     is more robust than either alone — like getting a second opinion.

  Q: How does it work offline?
  A: The entire trained neural network (14.8 MB) is bundled inside the APK
     file. TFLite runs the model directly on the phone's CPU. No data leaves
     the device. This is called "on-device machine learning."

  Q: Isn't 14.8 MB too big for an app?
  A: No — a typical WhatsApp photo is 1–3 MB; the model is 14.8 MB which is
     very reasonable for a deep learning model. We achieve this size through
     Float16 quantization which halves the model size with minimal accuracy loss.

  Q: What is Float16 quantization?
  A: By default, neural network weights are stored as 32-bit floats (4 bytes
     each). Float16 quantization converts them to 16-bit (2 bytes each), halving
     the file size. The accuracy loss is under 0.1% because the values are still
     precise enough for inference.

  Q: How accurate is it in real conditions?
  A: We tested on the standard PlantVillage benchmark test set (8,785 images)
     and achieved 98.3%. Real-world accuracy depends on photo quality — the app
     addresses this with its image quality gate that filters blurry/dark photos.

  Q: Could it be extended to more diseases?
  A: Yes. The architecture supports any number of output classes. To add new
     diseases, you would collect images, retrain the model (or fine-tune from
     our checkpoint), and update the TFLite file in the assets folder.

  Q: Why Flutter?
  A: Flutter allows writing ONE codebase that runs on Android (and iOS).
     It has excellent TFLite integration, fast UI rendering, and strong
     support for Material Design 3 components.

  Q: What about privacy?
  A: All processing is local. Photos are saved only to the device's storage
     for the scan history. No data is uploaded to any server.

  SECTION 14 — PAPER / REPORT ABSTRACT (ready to paste)
━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━

  Plant diseases pose a severe threat to agricultural productivity, causing
  20-40% of global crop yield loss annually. Timely identification is critical
  for effective disease management, yet expert resources are scarce in rural
  areas with limited internet connectivity. This paper presents AgroVision AI,
  an offline-capable Android application for real-time plant disease detection
  using on-device deep learning. We propose a dual-backbone convolutional neural
  network that combines EfficientNetB0 and MobileNetV2 feature extractors in
  parallel, concatenating their outputs before a compact classification head.
  The model is trained on the PlantVillage dataset (87,848 images, 38 classes,
  14 crop species) using a two-phase transfer learning strategy with mixed
  Float16 precision and a GPU-accelerated tf.data pipeline. After conversion
  to TFLite Float16 format (14.8 MB), the model achieves 98.3% accuracy on
  the 8,785-image test set with ~300–500 ms inference latency on commodity
  Android hardware. The application incorporates an image quality gate, severity
  classification, comprehensive treatment information, multilingual UI (English,
  Kannada, Hindi, Tamil, Telugu), and text-to-speech output — providing an
  end-to-end solution accessible to farmers regardless of language or
  internet availability.

  Keywords: plant disease detection, deep learning, EfficientNetB0,
            MobileNetV2, TFLite, on-device inference, Flutter, multilingual,
            PlantVillage, precision agriculture
